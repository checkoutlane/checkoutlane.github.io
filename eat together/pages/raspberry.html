BACK

The first time I encountered raspberries was not in their natural form, but in a jelly bean. On one of my first trips to the grocery store after I moved to the US, I followed my theory that red colored candy tends to taste good (except for Red Hots, never again). That day I fell down the rabbit hole of raspberry flavored foods: cookies, cakes, ice cream, anything. I’ve since rarely disliked a raspberry flavor, which has only deepened my trust for it. So it was fate that our current home already had raspberry bushes growing when we moved in.

The raspberry bushes in our backyard are prickly, fickle, and infested with Japanese beetles. Yet, I still wait expectantly for fruit each July, savoring every little berry straight off the canes. They don’t necessarily taste better than the store-bought ones, but seeing them grow day-by-day is what makes the ruby-colored berries more precious.

Anywhere I eat a raspberry, it takes me to sitting under an umbrella on my porch on a hot summer day, a handful of berries picked from the bush, still warm and translucent from the sunlight. It’s soft, first sweet, then tart, and once you get to the seeds, grassy and crunchy.
